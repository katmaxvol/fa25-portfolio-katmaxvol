An effective professional engineer is fundamentally a service provider: someone who translates complex technical ideas into decisions that serve stakeholders, clients, their team, and society. While technical competence is essential, and I am building these skills through summer internships and classes, engineering solutions rarely succeed on technical merit alone. As an undergraduate, I aim to intentionally develop non-technical skills that can help make me the best possible professional in my future chosen industry.

Professionalism, to me, centers on knowing oneâ€™s audience and how to most effectively communicate both technical and non-technical ideas to them. Explaining a design tradeoff to a colleague requires a different approach than presenting risk to management or constraints to a client, and learning the distinction between these approaches is something that schoolwork alone cannot provide. I can work on this skill as an undergraduate through leadership experiences and work opportunities, both on campus and during the summer through internships. It is through these types of higher-stakes projects and group efforts that these communication skills are tested and refined. I intend to pursue opportunities that place me in collaborative environments where outcomes depend on coordination and shared accountability rather than individual performance alone.

Another goal I have for my undergraduate years is to work on documenting and reflecting on engineering work. Clear documentation of design decisions, assumptions, and failures is critical for effective teamwork and long-term project success: it is much more efficient to learn from past mistakes and successes rather than repeating them over and over again. A wise person once told me that documentation of thought processes and design decisions can be an engineer's most important skill, and I definitely see the value in this advice. Through this portfolio and more consistent personal journaling, I aim to build and reinforce the habit of recording not just what I did, but why decisions were made and how they evolved from point A to point B. This practice encourages self-awareness, improves future decision-making, and supports collaboration with my peers.
